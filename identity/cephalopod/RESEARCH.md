# Source-specific sprite archive residue

**Status:** analytical capture. This document records what was visibly present in the referenced sprite-sheet and screen archives. It is not a recap of the design conversation, and it does not import third-party character geometry into the cephalopod mark.

## 1. Launch Octopus, Mega Man X, SNES

The sheet is organized as a dense pose field rather than a single definitive mascot view. The upper rows repeatedly change the relation among mantle, torso, and tentacles: arms compress close to the body, extend horizontally, rise independently, fan radially, and support airborne or attack states. The important residue is **pose-space diversity under persistent limb identity**. Tentacles remain distinguishable action-bearing components even when the overall silhouette changes substantially.

The bottom portion separates pale blue or white state silhouettes from the saturated red, yellow, and dark primary sprites. That is a second transferable residue: a console sheet can isolate a whole state family through palette assignment without changing the underlying recognition grammar.

Transfer: independent arm articulation, silhouette variation, explicit state-family palette separation. Reject: robotic armor, missiles, boss anatomy, red-and-yellow franchise palette, and any copied pose.

## 2. Octopus (Classic), Game & Watch Gallery 4, GBA

The classic octopus is carried almost entirely by flat black mass and the channels of light field between its arms. The sheet presents large action silhouettes and smaller temporal frames. Internal texture is nearly irrelevant. Recognition survives because each arm is a broad trajectory and because negative-space gaps keep adjacent limbs from merging.

Transfer: silhouette economy, arm-band separation, negative-space channels, and temporal variation. Reject: the exact Game & Watch animal silhouette, diver interaction, Nintendo expression, and LCD-character proportions.

## 3. Badniks, Sonic the Hedgehog 2, Genesis

The Badnik sheet is an archive of families rather than a single continuous scene. Enemies are grouped by zone, named, repeated across state rows, and accompanied by palette strips or detached components. The sheet makes assembly logic visible: a coherent sprite can be represented by a core, reusable modules, flipped or repeated tiles, and explicit palette roles. The octopus-tagged enemy is only one item in a larger modular corpus, so its character design is not the useful residue.

Transfer: family grouping, module boundaries, detached-part accounting, tile reuse, and palette-role documentation. Reject: robot anatomy, enemy coding, Sonic-specific colors, and Badnik silhouettes.

## 4. SimCity Budget Screen, SNES

The sheet places an assembled budget interface beside extracted components. Visible residue includes the complete beveled panel, multiple partial panel states, number and alphabet strips, repeated adjustment arrows, pointer graphics, bars, and small icons, all exposed on a key-color field. The sheet therefore records the relation **parts → state variants → assembled screen**. It is evidence that an authentic console graphic system can look strange because complete modules and extracted modules coexist on one page while remaining orderly.

Transfer: module inventory, repeated controls, glyph strips, nested panels, and co-location of parts with assembled state. Reject: literal beveled windows, fiscal labels, magenta key color as a compulsory palette, and copied UI ornaments.

## 5. SimCity map and information-screen residue

The game screen and archived map/UI material are planar systems. Roads, rails, power lines, zones, toolbars, information panels, and gaps are organized as a navigable network. Their meaningful unit is a junction, route, cell, overlay, or discontinuity. They do not form a vertical masonry barrier. This is the correction to the prior proof sheets: a right-side corruption field should behave as misregistered terrain, alternate route geometry, displaced cells, apertures, or an overlay state distributed through the square.

Transfer: route networks, junctions, gaps, overlaid panels, and planar tile state. Reject: brick walls, cliffs, defensive barriers, and a creature posed as if attacking an obstacle.

## 6. SimCity hidden debug screen

The debug screen holds several intact but incompatible modules on a cobalt field: a bounded residual picture, a separate message region, and monospaced menu rows. Its corruption quality comes from **module coexistence and wrong state composition**. Individual modules are still legible. Nothing is atomized into generic particles.

Transfer: layer collision, residual screen region, ordered text block, and rigid alignment. Reject: scanline smear, body corruption, random square clouds, and photographic glitch filters.

## Consequence for the icon search

The design space now has at least four independent axes: organism topology, negative-space economy, module assembly, and terrain-state failure. A valid proof sheet must vary those axes separately. Recoloring the same upright octopus or placing it repeatedly beside a vertical stack of tiles does not constitute a variant.
